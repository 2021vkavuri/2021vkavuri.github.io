2. Introduction¶

Religious Congregations serve as social and cultural pillars in the United States, offering community, support, and guidance to their adherents. This analysis aims to identify some factors that influence congregation size, and understand how these factors can vary based on geography, and over time.

Specifically, questions we seek to answer are: What features best predict a high adherence rate in a U.S. county? How does region affect religious affiliation patterns, particularly within Hindu demoninations?

Our primary analysis aims to look at features that best predict a high adherence rate.

Understanding these relationships helps better understand the role of these religious congregations on the general population, and can inform both general sociological discussions and policy decisions regarding these demographics.

3. Data Curation & Preprocessing¶

The data used in this project (Long_relig.csv) was sourced from a public dataset on religious congregations across the United States (from the Association of Religious Data Archives [ARDA]). It includes data from 1980 to 2010 like the number of congregations, adherents, religious families, as well as geographic data such as county and state.

Source: https://www.thearda.com/data-archive?fid=RCMSMGCY&tab=3

Hypothesis Test: Does region impact Hindu denomination?¶

We would first like to explore how some of the categorical variables in our data may influence each other. A specific example we can use is how geographical region (splitting states into NorthEast, South, MidWest, West), may impact Hindu denomination.

HO: In 2010, the region a Hindu adherent resides in does not have an effect on the likelihood of the religious group they affiliate with.

HA: In 2010, the region a Hindu adherent resides in does have an effect on the likelihood of the religious group they affiliate with.

In this hypothesis test, we are comparing two categorical variables. The first is the region, which we can derive from the US Census' division of states into regions. The second is the religious groups affiliated with the religion of Hinduism, which we can find by looking for religious groups with "Hindu" in the name.

Let's filter our dataset to only consider the relevant rows, as well as add a column with the associated region:

HO: The year does not have an effect on the proportion of adherents to the total population for a religious group

Since we want to use an ANOVA test, which assumes a normal distribution, we should check that first. We will take the aggregate adherent value for each group as a proportion of the population of the counties they are in. 5. Primary Analysis¶

What features best predict a high adherence rate?¶

Goal: Train classifiers on the data set and then, given they test well, look at feature importances to see which features of our data are the best predictors of high adherance rate. There are several features in out data that may strongly predict high adherence rates, all reviewed below.

Features we will use in X (input features):

Congreg: Number of congregations the group has. It is likely this will be the dominant feature, since in general it is intuitive to think a group with several congregations would have larger adherence numbers. However, we found searching for outliers that there are several groups with very high numbers of congregations but lower adherence rates (rural areas). There also may be large groups in heavily populated areas with few congregations but very high adherence numbers.

State: Encoded state abbreviaiton. It is also intuitive that certain states would be more relgious in general than others (The Bible Belt for example), so State would have some influence over number of adherents. Note: Adherence rate is taken as a proportion of population, as to not overweight states with high populations (which would naturally result in high adherence values).

FAMILY: Religious family category. Some relgious sects are larger than others, so once again it is intuitive that this would be a predictor of adherent numbers.

REL_TRAD: Religious tradition. Similar to FAMILY above, some religious traditions are more popular, and thus this may be a useful predictor of large adherent numbers.

All of these features may be strong predictors of a high adherance rate, so we will test Decision Tree and Random Forest classifiers to see which features our classifiers label as most useful. First we set up our data below.

Now we need to split and scale our data for each year, and test out models using k-fold cross-validation. We would like our classifiers to classify points with 'high' adherent values, so we need to define what 'high' means. Recall from the outlier search that the adherence column is skewed right. Because of this, a good way to split the data into 'high' and 'not high' values would be by simply comparing values to the mean of the column. Since the data is skewed right, the mean will have much fewer points to the right. So, splitting the data by being greater and or less than the mean provides a clean way of determining a 'high' adherent value while ensuring enough 'high' values for the classifiers to work. We should keep an eye on the split in Y below, as well as the True recall in our evaluation later.

7. Conclusion¶

This project provided an examination of religious congregations across the United States. We used data from 1980 to 2010 to explore patterns in geography, adherence, and demographic factors. Through data preprocessing, hypothesis testing, machine learning analysis, and visualization, we have found that:

  • The number of congregations in a county is the most significant predictor of high adherence rates
  • Geographic regions have a statistically significant impact on affiliation with Hindu groups, with notable variations across the Midwestern and Western regions.
  • The proportion of the population adhering to religious groups has not significantly changed over time, despite societal shifts.

Our Decision Tree and Random Forest classifiers demonstrated strong predictive abilities, with the congregation count consistently being the most important feature across all years, which is inline with intuition (more places of worship typically indicates more people attending the worships). To conclude, this project has given us a clearer understanding of religious adherence in the United States across different regions and times.
